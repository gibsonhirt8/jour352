Data Visualization: The effect of home court advantage in college basketball

Gibson Hirt,   JOUR 352

I believe that there is a strong and noticeable correlation in college basketball between home court advantage, winning percentage and scoring margin. Most people would agree that teams typically play better when at home in front of their owns fans, but does the data actually support that? I took a look at statistics from all Power Four (ACC, Big Ten, Big 12, SEC) schools from the 2024-25 men's college basketball season. And unsurprisingly, the public's hunch was correct: teams simply aren't the same when playing on the road. The pie chart seen to the right breaks things down perfectly. A staggering 43 of these teams have a home winning percentage at least .300 higher than on the road. Being that strong at home can be the difference between being an NCAA tournament lock and fighting for your life on the bubble come Selection Sunday.

The team-by-team comparisons in the bar chart and scatterplot provide even more data to support my argument. The blue dots, the home winning percentages, are clustered to the right while the red dots, the away winning percentages, are stuck to the left. It's important to note that it's not just the blue-blood programs like Duke, Kansas or Kentucky who dominant at home, but also middle-of-the-pack programs like Colorado show meaningful separation in their success at home vs away. The consistent disparity tells me that college basketball is largely shaped by travel and fan environments.

I also wanted to take a deeper dive at how this trend has affected the Maryland men's basketball program over years, as I consider myself a diehard fan. I looked at the Terps' scoring margins in and away from College Park dating all the away back to the 2001-02 season. Over the last two decades, Maryland has almost always had a double-digit scoring margin at home, while its away margins typically hover under five or even in the negatives. It's important to note that this isn't only occurring in down years; this pattern has held true during peak seasons like 2009-10 season and last year's Crab Five squad -- with the only exception being the incredible 2001-02 team that won the National Championship. This long-term consistency, both at Maryland and across Division I, is why I think that home court advantage is not a temporary trend but a fundamental part of college basketball.
